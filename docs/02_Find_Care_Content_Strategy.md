### TL;DR

  accessibility, and responsive implementation.

### 1 · CONTEXT — *what & who*
**Section title:** A portal that assumed users knew what they were looking for.

The Find Care portal helps members on employer-sponsored health plans find care, understand
coverage, and manage benefits. It's one of the most-used features on the platform — and used to be
one of the most frustrating. The existing design leaned on **keyword search.** That works great if
you're a clinician typing a CPT code. It works poorly if you're a person who just found out your
knee hurts and needs to know who to see and what it'll cost.

---

### 2 · DISCOVERY & RESEARCH — *the process beat — ADD REAL DEPTH HERE*
**Section eyebrow:** Research
**Section title:** Following the members who gave up.

> This is the section to strengthen most. Right now the study jumps to the insight. Show the *work*
> that produced it. Use the methods you actually ran — fill the brackets with specifics.

The bounce rates already told a story: members were landing on search, and leaving without a result.
The question was *why.* I looked at [**search-log patterns / session data / usability sessions —
confirm which you ran**] and paid attention to the exact words members typed into the box.

The pattern was unmistakable. They weren't searching for providers or procedures — they were typing
their *problem.* "Knee." "How much." "Is this covered." The interface was built to answer a query;
members were asking a question. **Most members didn't know what to search for, because the thing they
needed wasn't a search term — it was a starting point.**

### 3 · INSIGHT — *the reframe*
**Section eyebrow:** The insight
**Section title:** One observation that changed everything.

> **The core finding: members don't want to *search*. They want to *find*.**

Search implies you already know what you're looking for. Most members didn't. They had a concern
("my knee hurts"), not a query ("orthopedic consultation, CPT 99243"). The interface had to meet them
where they were — not where the data structure expected them to be. **That reframe was the whole case
study.** Everything else followed from it.

---

### 4 · DECISIONS — *ideation → three moves*
**Section eyebrow:** Design moves
**Section title:** Three changes that followed from the insight.

**01 · Categories first, search second.** Browse-by-category became the primary path — organized
around how members talk about their needs, not how data is structured in the back end. Keyword search
is still there for members who want it, just not in the driver's seat.

**02 · An assistant for the uncertain.** Some members don't have the vocabulary to start. A
conversational Find Care assistant handles the "I don't know where to begin" cases — asking questions
in plain language, steering members toward the right care path, with cost and coverage visible along
the way.

**03 · Mobile-first, not mobile-also.** More than 70% of members were already on mobile; the old
design was desktop-first, shoehorned down. The redesign started from the phone — touch targets sized
for thumbs, bottom navigation for one-handed use, collapsible filters that don't block content.

### 6 · OUTCOME — *with structure, and one number if you have it*
**Section eyebrow:** Outcome
**Section title:** What changed.

**Fewer dead-end searches.** Category navigation replaced empty keyword results with a path forward
— members who used to hit "no results" now land on a route to care they didn't have to guess at.
*[If you have even a directional abandonment/retention figure you can defend, it lands here — the one
place a number sharpens the whole study.]*

**Multiple paths to one destination.** Category browsing, keyword search, and the assistant all lead
to care options — three routes for three kinds of user, instead of one route that assumed expertise.

**WCAG 2.1 AA across the flow.** Contrast, screen-reader support, and focus-management gaps in the
old portal were systematically resolved. Every flow in the redesign passes AA.

**Mobile that matches usage.** Touch targets, thumb-zone primary actions, and bottom-sheet filters
replaced the desktop-first compromise.

**A reusable component library.** The work produced search bars, provider cards, cost-estimation
patterns, and filter controls — reused across adjacent features and modules.

---

### 7 · REFLECTION — *seniority*
**Section title:** Designing for low technical literacy.

The instinct when users struggle is to **simplify by removing features.** The better move is usually
the opposite: provide multiple paths to the same destination — category browsing, keyword search, and
the assistant all lead to care, they just let different users arrive differently. And transparency
mattered more than I expected: when members can see what they'll pay before booking, they're more
likely to seek care at all. **Hidden costs create anxiety. Visible costs create confidence.**
