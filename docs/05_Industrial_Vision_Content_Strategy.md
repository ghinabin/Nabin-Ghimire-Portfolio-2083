# Industrial Vision — Content Strategy & Copy

## Strategy (read first)

**Role in portfolio:** Range + craft + longevity + the *origin story* of your systems thinking.
This is a "secondary/other work" piece — keep it shorter than the flagships — but it does three jobs
no product case study can: it shows a genuine **visual/typographic eye**, it proves you've sustained
and led a real thing for nearly a decade, and it lets you say "I was building design systems before I
had the words for it."

**Who's reading & what they check:** Anyone questioning whether a systems/enterprise designer can
still *design*. This is where you answer that. It's also humanizing — a real magazine, real
advertisers, real research.

**The one thing this study proves:** Craft and systems thinking aren't separate for you — a
consistent visual identity held across nine editions *is* a design system.

**Narrative arc:** A student magazine that had to look credible next to paid industry ads and dense
research → I built a grid/type system and a cover identity that survives new themes every year → nine
editions, one recognizable DNA, print extended to digital.

**The UX process, made visible (this is the spine):**
`Brief (credibility bar: real ads + real research) → Problem (dense research must stay readable) →
System (grid + type + cover identity) → Apply (re-themed each edition) → Extend (print→PDF) →
Outcome (9 editions) → Reflection (my first design system)`
The "process" here is a *systems* process — building rules once and letting them carry different
content. Frame it that way; it directly reinforces your Viveka positioning.

### 1 · CONTEXT — *what & who*
**Section eyebrow:** Context
**Section title:** A real magazine, not a class handout.

Industrial Vision is SOIESNEPAL's print magazine for the Industrial Engineering community at
Thapathali Campus — read by students, faculty, alumni, and industry partners, and funded in part by
advertising from industrial businesses. Every edition carries original research and articles under one
tagline: **Innovation, Industry, Prosperity.** That combination — real research, paying advertisers, a
readership that includes professionals — meant the design had to earn its place next to the content.
I've been the Design Lead and Art Director shaping that identity since IV 2, in 2016.

---

### 2 · THE PROBLEM — *the design tension*
**Section eyebrow:** The core problem
**Section title:** Dense engineering research doesn't read itself.

> **Pull quote:** "The tagline was the easy part. Making the research readable was the job."

Every edition mixes long-form research papers, technical articles, and interviews with industry
professionals — substantive content that isn't naturally inviting. The design had to make that
genuinely readable for a student audience while still looking credible enough to sit next to paid
industry advertising. Neither side could win at the other's expense.

---

### 3 · DESIGN MOVES — *the system*
**Section eyebrow:** Design moves
**Section title:** Three decisions behind the identity.

**01 · A layout system built for dense content.** A grid and type hierarchy in InDesign that makes
long-form research and interviews as readable as they are credible — not just another article dump.

**02 · A cover identity strong enough to carry the tagline.** One consistent wordmark and cover
language across editions, re-themed each year — instantly recognizable as Industrial Vision, never a
repeat.

**03 · Print + digital, so each edition outlives its print run.** Pairing a downloadable PDF with each
print run keeps the research reachable for alumni and industry readers long after physical copies run
out.

### 7 · REFLECTION — *the connective tissue to your product work*
**Section eyebrow:** Reflection
**Section title:** The first design system I ever built — I just didn't call it one yet.

Keeping one visual identity credible across nearly a decade, through new themes and new content every
year, was my earliest lesson in systems thinking: build the rules once, then let them carry very
different content without losing what makes it recognizable. It's the same instinct behind the design
system work I do professionally today — years before I had a name for it.
