## Strategy (read first)

**Role in portfolio:** Your **range + adaptability** piece. Secondary/"other work" — keep it shorter
than the flagships. It proves three things a healthcare/enterprise portfolio otherwise can't: you can
work in **ambiguity**, learn **unfamiliar tools** fast (Unity, Cinema 4D, Substance Painter), and
operate across **two disciplines** (product design + 3D art) on one deliverable. It's the concrete
version of the "growth mindset" every enterprise team claims to want.

**Who's reading & what they check:** Anyone gauging whether you're curious and adaptable, or a
one-lane specialist. Also a good signal for AI-augmented/next-gen design teams (Omne-type roles) —
you're comfortable prototyping something that doesn't exist yet.

**The one thing this study proves:** You can take a vague, timely idea and drive it to a *working*
prototype — and you're honest about what a prototype is for.

**Narrative arc:** COVID killed in-person events → we asked whether a virtual world could actually
solve that → I framed the MVP, then took it sketch → greybox → full-fidelity → a playable
multiplayer prototype → concluded honestly as R&D.

**The UX process, made visible (this is the spine — and it's already strong here):**
`Frame (Five Ws MVP exercise) → Sketch → Greybox in Unity (test scale/flow) → Full-fidelity
environments → Features → Honest R&D outcome`
This is your best-documented *process* of all six. The sketch→greybox→fidelity progression is
textbook iterative prototyping — lean into it and show the progression visually.

### TL;DR
- **Challenge.** COVID lockdowns made showcasing products or hosting events in person impossible — a
  real problem for businesses with no way to demonstrate value.
- **My role.** Product Designer & 3D Artist on a 5-person R&D team — owned the UX process end-to-end
  and built environment art in Cinema 4D and Substance Painter.
- **Outcome.** A working multiplayer VR prototype in Unity — exhibition halls, a 1,000+ capacity
  concert venue, portal navigation, voice and text chat — proven out as an internal demo.

---

### 1 · CONTEXT — *why this, why now*
**Section eyebrow:** Context
**Section title:** A blockchain company's bet on the metaverse.

EKbana had already built crypto wallet and blockchain products by the time the pandemic hit. When
Facebook renamed itself Meta in October 2021 and announced $10 billion in metaverse investment, the
timing lined up with a problem we'd already seen: businesses couldn't hold events, run exhibitions, or
showcase products in person. We formed a 5-person team to explore whether a virtual world could
actually solve that — not just look impressive in a demo reel. We landed on virtual exhibitions and
events as the wedge: a space businesses could rent, with a longer-term vision of parks, concert
venues, and more as the idea proved out.

---

### 2 · PROCESS — *the process beat — this is your strongest*
**Section eyebrow:** Process
**Section title:** From a Q&A framework to a working build.

> This is textbook iterative prototyping. Keep it front and center, and show the progression.

**01 · Framing the MVP with a Five Ws exercise.** Rather than guessing at scope, we ran a structured
Five Ws and How process to define exactly what an MVP virtual exhibition needed — before any pixel or
polygon.

**02 · Sketch to greybox, fast.** Ideas moved from pen-and-paper sketches straight into Unity
greyboxing — testing scale, navigation, and flow in 3D before investing in final art.

**03 · Full-fidelity environments, not just blockouts.** Once the layout worked, I modeled the
environments in Cinema 4D and textured them in Substance Painter and Photoshop — turning a greybox
into a space that felt real enough to hold an event in.

### 3 · WHAT WE BUILT — *features*
**Section eyebrow:** What we built
**Section title:** A social platform, not just a 3D showroom.

**01 · Multiplayer exploration.** Join a shared server and move through the world alongside other
avatars, with navigation inspired by Decentraland and open-world games.

**02 · Portals between spaces.** Sci-fi-styled portal gates with a swirl transition move users between
the exhibition hall, concert venue, and outdoor scene without breaking immersion.

**03 · Group chat & voice call.** Text and voice built in, so the space functioned as a real social
platform, not a static showroom.

**04 · In-world commerce.** Products showcased in the exhibition hall could be browsed and purchased
directly within the virtual interface.

### 5 · OUTCOME — *honest R&D framing*
**Section eyebrow:** Outcome
**Section title:** What the prototype proved.

- **A working build, not a concept deck.** A playable multiplayer prototype in Unity — the idea
  proven with something people could walk through, not a rendering.
- **A venue built to hold a real crowd.** A concert hall designed for 1,000+ concurrent users, with
  automated lift and stairs to a balcony level.
- **Two disciplines, one deliverable.** Full ownership across UX/product design and hands-on 3D art —
  research, prototyping, and environment modeling on the same project.
- **A proof-of-concept, honestly labeled.** Concluded as an internal demo rather than a public launch
  — it did what R&D is supposed to do: answer the question before betting more on it.

---

### 6 · REFLECTION — *seniority through honesty*
**Section eyebrow:** Reflection
**Section title:** Not every prototype has to ship to be worth building.

Working across product design and 3D art on the same project forced a discipline I hadn't needed
before — a feature only mattered once it existed in the space, not just on a whiteboard. Nepverse
never went public, and that's fine: its job was to answer whether the idea worked at all, and it did
that clearly enough to be worth the year it took.
